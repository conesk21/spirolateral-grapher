Spirolateral Graphs

Discovered in the 60's by a teenager with graph paper and a particularly monotonic teacher, spirolateral graphs epitomize a peculiar joy in mathematics, when a simple set of rules begets unexpected complexity and beauty. In general, spirolateral graphs are polygons generated from a sequence of integers - tradionally the numbers from 1 to n - and a turning angle. From these starting elements, one simply draws a straight line as long as the first element of the sequence then turns according the specified angle before drawing another line as long as the next element in the sequence, turing again, drawing another line, and so forth until the shape is closed or the drawer runs out of patience (or graph paper). These shapes are enthralling, particularly because they are easy to draw, but rife with variation and beauty. If you use this tool, use it as Frank C. Odds would have wanted - following absent-minded curiousity!

This particular graphing tool is engineered to draw "modern spirolaterals" with turning angle 90. Modern spirolaterals are those generated from any permutation of integers (not only the numbers from 1 to n) so go crazy with your personal integer selection! If you are of the pattern-finding sort, you can also look into the closure properties (which graphs form a closed shape, and which do not) or the "bounding-box" size for a particular graph. A bounding-box is the smallest square with integer coordinates which can contain the graph in its entirety, and even a different ordering of the same integers can form a radically larger/smaller bounding box!
